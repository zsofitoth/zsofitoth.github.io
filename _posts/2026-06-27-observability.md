# Observability

## Motivation

I am spending some time reflecting on software engineering fundamentals by reading books and thinking back on my time spent in the industry developing software. I wanted to start with observability (a topic that is very dear to my heart) so I picked up a book called [Observability Engineering](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/observability-engineering/9781492076438/) (1st edition) in the hope that it would help me articulate some of my points better and simply just to learn more about observability.

The other motivation behind this post is my opinion that quality should still be important in 2026. In my experience many organizations are struggling with the concept of quality and how to best ensure it. The discussion is always about how to keep delivering value and move fast and how much we should invest in quality while still staying pragmatic. A lot of people treat this as some big tradeoff where there is no right answer, which I partially agree with. Testing more, being Agile, hiring QA engineers, investing in observability, defensive programming, these are all areas where quality can be ensured not in a mutually exclusive way. Some are constrained by budget, others are by time to delivery.

There is also a lot of discourse around developing with coding agents and in general about "where we are headed". I would like to firstly say, I am not even gonna pretend like I understand where we are headed nor will I attempt to predict where we'll be in 6 months. However, I find the following quite intriguing:
> "Now that coding is _largely_ solved, the bottleneck moves to [insert LinkedIn post here about where the bottleneck moves to]"

To me one of the bottlenecks even before genAI was verification especially in production which is something that was never trivial, except we produced way less in quantity so there was a ceiling to what we needed to verify and how many bugs a week we shipped to production. Also comprehension and the ability to keep up with the changes and the state of the codebase, especially if one worked on a product long enough kept teams afloat. Even if I was new and hadn't yet built the mental models or acquired the necessary domain knowledge fast, chances were someone else in the team would surely know how to put out a fire in production and save our asses. Don't get me wrong, this was highly annoying and stressful in the good old days as well, but things have been speeding up and more lines of code are being added than ever before. I'm assuming if developers are no longer spending their time on shipping features (because agents are doing that) then that must mean the tradeoffs we've always made when it comes to quality might have to be rethought as well. And that would mean we get to go to places we have rarely been before and we have the chance to raise the bar.

With that being said let's talk about why observability is relevant and worth caring about.

## Observability vs Monitoring

I want to start with something I have also recently learned by reading [Observability Engineering](https://www.oreilly.com/library/view/observability-engineering/9781492076438/) (1st edition) and that is clearing up the difference between terms. To be perfectly honest, I used to use the terms _observability_ and _monitoring_ interchangeably and thought they meant the same thing, but as it turns out not even close.

**Observability** is a measure of how well you can understand and explain any state your system can get into - given the outputs you can measure, can you infer the complete internal state of the system. This is the strict definition in control theory (Rudolf E. Kálmán), in software it basically means if the system gets into a weird state in production, can the person working that day understand how badly users are impacted (what is the userflow that's impacted) and ask questions to investigate why it is happening? This is of course without adding any code changes (extra instrumentation) or calling the longest serving member of the team on their day off.

If observability is about investigating unknown-unknowns, **monitoring** is about known-unknowns and is fundamentally reactive meaning we anticipate things going wrong and try to guess what those will be and why they would happen. A great example here is an incident in production happens, all hands are on board trying to figure out why. A certain amount of time passes, a lot of guessing and "intuition" is involved, finally the engineer with enough institutional knowledge arrives to save the day and "guesses right". This outage then will be remembered as something bad, if lucky the root cause is discussed in a postmortem meeting where engineers set arbitrary alerts to monitor this exact condition and maybe some other preventative action points will be noted down (but most likely never realized). Then rinse and repeat. There is also a good chance that the same incident will not even happen in years, but occasionally the alert only the selected few know the full context of will pick up false positives.

I kept the scenario above terribly oversimplified yet it should feel familiar. This is not to say monitoring is not useful, the book makes a good case about certain things that are still worth monitoring this way (infrastructure is an example). More on this below.

### Two ways to alert

I would also like to introduce something called signals (types of telemetry data) briefly to further build on the definitions above. Metrics, logs, traces are the most well known and used ones, but they are not the only ones. Some other known signals are exceptions and errors (think of Sentry), profiling data and product analytics (Mixpanel), but there are many more.

In the monitoring use cases, people would often pick a custom metric and set off some alerts based on arbitrary threshold. An example would be an alert when CPU on the pipeline workers exceeds 85% for 5 minutes. High CPU often means nothing is wrong that would be impacting users, but could indicate something is underprovisioned. 

Observability works with structured events (traces) and structured logs. Alerts are based on service level objectives (SLOs) rather than thresholds. These must reflect urgent user impact and must require investigation rather than immediate action. I always think of an ex-coworker I regard highly here, who said when he worked at a well-known e-commerce company one of the objectives was that customers browsing products (shoes, accessories) would never have to wait longer than 200ms for the page to load. If it took longer over some amount of time in a month (error budget was exceeded), then an important user journey was impacted and someone had to investigate the root cause. The difference here might not be immediately obvious, but instead of setting some threshold based alert that looks for anomalies in underlying infrastructure, it is very much about a user journey impacted. Meaning I won't get an alert about something like CPU usage in `pod A` is out of control, even if that is the root cause. This is useful, because the _what_ and the _why_ are decoupled and now I can start asking questions about what might be wrong and verify/disprove my assumptions by looking at logs and traces. Ideally important code paths are instrumented (this should no longer be an issue as agentic coding tools do a decent job at the mechanical part of adding spans) and sampling rates are set high enough. This now allows any engineer to compare requests, events and their different conditions (think of feature flags, host name) to each other and get to a state close enough to reproduce it locally or roll back a change.

I am conflicted whether I always want to draw a hard line between these two, a fair use case of monitoring could be looking at a data pipeline where we periodically process some incoming data and then load that to a datastore. A useful metric to set up here could be the last time the data was pushed to the storage and if it was longer than 24 hours then an alert should fire off and someone should investigate why. I have set metrics like these up before and they provided value. The question is whether this can be formed better as an SLO or if it even makes sense to go down that road, but this is why these things are not easy and the general rule of thumb doesn't always hold.

### Structured events

I want to talk more about structured events (traces and spans), what characterizes them and why they help with the investigation part (how they make it possible to deep dive).

Asking any question about combination of telemetry details, requires the ability to arbitrarily slice and dice data along any number of dimensions. A structured event is a record of one unit of work: everything that happened while a request passed through a service, captured as a single object with a bunch of fields attached. This is a span. A trace is just the connected set of spans for one request's full path across services, stitched together by a shared trace ID. So when I talk about slicing and dicing, the event (the span) is the unit I'm slicing.

The event is characterized by high dimensionality and should support high cardinality. Dimensionality is how many fields/attributes an event carries, these can be `user_id`, `request_id`, `region`, `feature_flags`, `host`, etc. Cardinality is how many distinct values a given field can take, `user_id` is high-cardinality because it has millions of unique values; `region` is low-cardinality because the number of values is not more than a handful.

The reason both matter together: to go from "something is slow" to "*this* user's requests on *this* build in *this* region are slow," you need many dimensions to ask along, and you need to put high-cardinality fields on those axes to actually narrow it down. The more of this you capture up front, the easier it is to dive in and ask why the system got into a weird state and how users are experiencing it in production. This is possible without shipping new instrumentation first.

Metrics are bad at high cardinality due to how they are physically stored. A metric is stored as one time series per unique combination of label values, and each series is a separate object the backend has to allocate, index and keep in memory. Cardinality multiplies: if you've got `endpoint` × `status` × `region` you might have a few thousand series, but the moment you add `user_id` you multiply by a million and your monitoring backend falls over (I've watched custom metrics get quite expensive this way).

So metrics force you to choose your dimensions up front and throw away the per-request detail, which means the high-cardinality fields you'd actually want for debugging are the exact ones you can't afford to keep. A structured event does the opposite: it keeps every field on the raw event and lets you aggregate at query time instead of write time. With metrics you can only answer questions you anticipated when you defined the labels; with events you can ask a new question — "is this specific to users who hit the new checkout flag?" — after the fact, because the raw rows are still there.

One thing to note about the volume of structured events though, at scale you can't keep every event, so you sample. The thing to know is that good sampling isn't uniform, it biases toward keeping the interesting events (errors, slow requests, anything unusual) and downsamples the successful ones. So you're not actually giving up the investigative power, you're keeping the events you'd want during an incident and dropping the ones you'd never look at. How exactly you do that is a topic of its own, so I'll leave it here.

## Why care about observability?

If the motivation section didn't convince you then I will continue by saying observability and monitoring positively contribute to continuous delivery (see this [DORA article](https://dora.dev/capabilities/monitoring-and-observability/)). The book also makes the case that caring about culture and people is a good reason to invest in observability. I have my own take on this, I've seen firsthand how having observability set up makes all the difference for diversity and inclusion.

### Culture

_Disclaimer: this section is from my experience in the industry, not from studies._

What I have seen many times is that culture drives quality, because it drives engagement. Engaged people stay, notice things, own problems, don't check out — disengaged people do the bare minimum. When an environment exists where team members feel a certain sense of belonging and they feel like they are succeeding and contributing, it is less likely they will start coping by emotionally and mentally checking out. 

The hero culture undermines this as it creates an environment that puts an unnecessary amount of pressure on one person to solve all problems and incidents, while making the other team members feel useless. The hero here is the engineer with the most institutional knowledge of the system, this is the engineer who gets called on their day off because there is an emergency and everyone is scared when they go on vacation. This obviously leads to burnout and isn't sustainable for so many reasons.

This is exactly what good observability dissolves. When the system can be interrogated — when anyone can ask "what changed, for which users, on which build" and get an answer from the data instead of from the one person who remembers — the institutional knowledge stops being a person and starts being a property of the system. The questions the hero used to answer from memory become questions anyone on the team can ask. That's what democratized access actually means: not everyone is equally experienced, but everyone can equally investigate.

I want to specifically talk about diversity and inclusion which I know no longer gets the spotlight it used to. Hiring women and minorities is no longer tied to KPIs, but that doesn't make diverse teams any less important. Women/minorities are structurally less likely to be the long-tenured insider with institutional knowledge, so retention can really be aided by setting up observability well because then sharing responsibilities becomes a reality and that is a powerful way to build belonging and allowing people to succeed. 

So one thing I have seen before, is hiring women for the sake of hiring women and then abandoning them and letting them figure things out without any support. Often there is no malice in it, teams are stretched thin, stressed out and individual contributors are not allowed to allocate the time necessary to spend with new hires. This then leads to a lot of imposter syndrome and self doubt, a lot of internalization about failing in the industry and also withdrawal. Then people would get mad about why the quota even exists and "why are they forced to hire women". This issue is really complex and there is a lot more to say about it. Not feeling like belonging and then withdrawing doesn't only affect women, but I have seen female coworkers who were set up to fail leave the industry, even though they were capable and brought a lot to the team.

Investing in observability is also a strong use case for onboarding new people faster, but the already experienced and confident new joiners will have an easier time figuring fragmented systems out and pushing through the pain, but they will certainly be grateful if they don't have to.

### Personal Anecdote

Some time ago, I worked with a tech lead who liked to move fast, break things and not write tests. Once I confronted him about not writing any tests and things constantly breaking in production and he rebuffed me by saying: overtesting is bad and slows the pipelines down. He thought the same about typechecks, so he removed the build step from the CI/CD pipeline and each time I pulled the code it was full of TypeScript build errors. I have to also add we had an okay observability setup, so at least we noticed when critical journeys were broken. Nonetheless, it wasn't nice when the same thing broke prod 3 times in different ways in a single day because adding tests was an afterthought.

So what is overtesting and is it bad? It refers to adding too many tests where some of them might not be useful. It is very much real, but I used to laugh it off by saying, you can barely convince people to write any tests so this only exists on paper, but not in reality.

I haven't thought about overtesting in a very long time, until I started working a new job where I quickly realized why overtesting is a problem and when it occurs. As the observability setup was really noisy and quite unusable for anyone who hasn't worked at the company for 5+ years, most developers considered PR reviews and writing tests the only feedback loops. This included me, after a PR went out to production I would test it manually once if it was straight forward enough.

I also had to rely on the reviewer doing a thorough job reviewing my changes. I always wanted to request the same person to review my PRs, he did the best job of noticing when something was off and I felt safe when he approved my PRs. This also meant my PRs would stay open for days or weeks and I would add very extensive integration tests. We constantly had issues with flaky tests and we had a rotation every sprint where we would either disable flaky tests or fix them if they were easy enough to fix. In the meantime we had nearly no visibility of how customers were experiencing a feature in production. We didn't know what percentile had issues with timeouts or were experiencing slowness (the default GraphQL endpoint timeout was set to 30 seconds for all endpoints). We were basically blind, frustrated and just hoping for the best.

To most companies brand reputation means a lot. I can't tell you how many incident triage rooms I sat in on a Friday afternoon with 5 other teams trying to figure out how to put out incidents that were PR (public relations) disasters. For sure more than I would have liked to, but these things happen. What makes a difference is whether I sit in that room for one hour and we identify the root cause or I'm sitting there for 5 hours. When I was in there for 5 hours, it pretty much spiked my stress hormone levels enough for me to spend the whole weekend recovering from it. These were the times when I thought about, there is a better way, but then why is it such a darn hard sell?

#### Hardest part

Convincing people it's worth spending time on a good observability setup and justifying the business need are the hardest and most frustrating things. Most people understand the problem, want something to be done about it and then it stops there. My best guess is that in most people's heads, it's some abstract thing that will pay off in the far future and we just have to get through today or the quarter. Others (I used to belong in this group) just don't know where to begin.

What ends up happening though (I've seen this at two distinct companies), someone who had enough and has set up observability tools before somewhere else just adds capabilities of instrumentation and takes care of the infrastructure part, so now the possibility is there for building more observable systems. This is not great, not terrible. Not terrible because now we at least have something to make our lives easier and it's much better than nothing, certainly an improvement over the 46 pages of Sentry exceptions. Not great because the culture around it will never take off and it's so much more about the culture and mindset than about the infrastructure and the capability. I would argue observability should be treated as a product that comes with infrastructure rather than just some developer tool. I would love if product managers could participate and make product decisions around it. In the meantime, I am eternally grateful for the people who go in and make an attempt to set something up and educate people about better ways. This is also how I've been exposed to the concept and wanted to learn more about it.
